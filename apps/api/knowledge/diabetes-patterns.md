# Common Diabetes Patterns

## Dawn Phenomenon
The dawn phenomenon is a natural rise in blood glucose that occurs in the early morning hours, typically between 4:00 AM and 8:00 AM. It is caused by the body's release of growth hormone, cortisol, and catecholamines, which increase hepatic glucose production and reduce insulin sensitivity.

Management strategies:
- Increase basal rate during early morning hours (if using a pump)
- Control-IQ and similar algorithms can partially compensate with increased basal delivery
- Correction factors may need to be stronger in the morning to account for reduced insulin sensitivity
- The magnitude varies by individual and can change seasonally

## Post-Meal Glucose Spikes
Post-meal (postprandial) glucose excursions are common in Type 1 diabetes. Blood glucose typically begins rising 15-30 minutes after eating and peaks at 60-90 minutes.

Contributing factors:
- Carbohydrate quantity and glycemic index of the meal
- Timing of the pre-meal bolus (bolusing 15-20 minutes before eating reduces spike magnitude)
- Fat and protein content (high-fat meals delay glucose absorption, causing a prolonged rise)
- Current insulin on board and correction factor adequacy
- Carb ratio accuracy for the time of day

## Insulin Stacking
Insulin stacking occurs when additional insulin doses are taken before previous doses have fully acted. Since rapid-acting insulin has a 3-5 hour duration, taking a correction dose within 2-3 hours of a previous dose risks hypoglycemia.

Key points:
- Insulin on board (IoB) must be considered before any correction dose
- Control-IQ and similar algorithms factor IoB into automated corrections
- Manual corrections should subtract active IoB from the calculated dose
- Pump bolus calculators typically account for IoB but users who override may stack

## Exercise Effects on Blood Glucose
Physical activity affects blood glucose through multiple mechanisms:
- Aerobic exercise: typically lowers blood glucose during and after activity
- Anaerobic exercise (weight lifting, sprinting): may temporarily raise blood glucose due to stress hormone release
- The glucose-lowering effect can persist for 24-48 hours after exercise
- Insulin sensitivity increases during and after exercise, meaning less insulin is needed

Management strategies:
- Reduce basal rate 30-60 minutes before planned exercise
- Consider temporary basal rate reduction of 50-80% during exercise
- Monitor closely for delayed hypoglycemia 4-8 hours after exercise
- Control-IQ Exercise Mode reduces target range aggressiveness

## Overnight Glucose Management
Overnight glucose control is critical for overall diabetes management. Common patterns include:
- Bedtime highs: often from dinner carb underestimation or insufficient dinner bolus
- Midnight lows: may indicate basal rate is too high in early sleep hours
- Dawn phenomenon rise: natural hormone-driven rise in early morning hours
- Compression lows: sleeping on CGM sensor can cause false low readings

Control-IQ Sleep Mode:
- Tightens target range to 112.5-120 mg/dL (vs standard 112.5-160 mg/dL)
- Does NOT deliver automated correction boluses (basal adjustments only)
- Increases basal insulin when predicted glucose exceeds 120 mg/dL (lower threshold than standard mode's 160 mg/dL)
- Reduces basal insulin when predicted glucose drops below 112.5 mg/dL
- Should be scheduled for typical sleep hours in pump settings

## Time in Range (TIR) Targets
The international consensus on Time in Range (2019 Advanced Technologies & Treatments for Diabetes):
- Target range: 70-180 mg/dL
- Time in range goal: >70% (approximately 16 hours 48 minutes per day)
- Time below range (<70 mg/dL): <4% (approximately 58 minutes per day)
- Time below 54 mg/dL: <1% (approximately 14 minutes per day)
- Time above range (>180 mg/dL): <25%
- Time above 250 mg/dL: <5%
- Average glucose target: approximately 154 mg/dL (corresponds to HbA1c ~7.0%)
- Glucose Management Indicator (GMI): target <7.0%
- Coefficient of variation (CV): target <36% (indicates stable glucose)
